01 — Foundation

What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities — your bank, your employer, even your friends — to trick you into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or OTPs.

Unlike malware that exploits software flaws, phishing exploits human psychology. It works because our brains are wired to trust authority, respond to urgency, and give people the benefit of the doubt.

📧

Email Phishing

The classic: fake emails impersonating banks, services, or colleagues with malicious links or attachments.

📱

Smishing

SMS-based attacks. A fake "delivery failed" or "OTP verification" text that leads to credential theft.

📞

Vishing

Voice phishing. Callers pose as tech support, bank officers, or government agents to extract information.

🎯

Spear Phishing

Targeted attacks crafted using personal details scraped from LinkedIn or social media for higher success rates.

02 — Spot the Red Flags

Dissect a Real Phishing Email

This is a reconstructed example of a real phishing email. Hover over the highlighted parts to see what's wrong, then reveal all hints.

From: security@paypa1-support.com SPOOFED
To: you@youremail.com
Date: Mon, 28 Jul 2025 03:17:44 +0000 ODD TIME
Urgent: Your PayPal account has been SUSPENDED

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your PayPal account. To avoid permanent suspension, you must verify your identity within 24 hours.

Click the button below to restore your account:

Verify Account Now →

If you did not request this, ignore this email. Your account will be permanently closed in 24 hours.

Regards,
PayPal Security Team

1
Sender domain is paypa1-support.com — the number "1" replaces the letter "l". Always read domain names character by character.
2
Email sent at 3 AM UTC — attackers operate in different time zones and use bots; legitimate companies send from business hours.
3
Urgent language ("SUSPENDED", "24 hours") creates panic so you act before you think — real services give you time to respond.
4
Hovering the button reveals it goes to paypa1-verify-secure.ru (.ru = Russia), not paypal.com. Always check URLs before clicking.
5
Signed "PayPal Security Team" not your name — real PayPal emails always include your registered name as a personalization signal.
03 — Attacker Playbook

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishers exploit psychological principles that work on almost everyone. Knowing the playbook is your best defence.

URGENCY

Creating False Urgency

"Your account expires in 2 hours." Panic bypasses rational thinking.

Attackers impose fake deadlines to prevent you from pausing and verifying. Real services never require immediate action under threat. If something feels rushed, that's your signal to slow down — not speed up. Always verify through the company's official website or phone number.
AUTHORITY

Impersonating Authority

Faking your CEO, bank, or government to trigger automatic compliance.

We're socially conditioned to obey authority figures. A "CEO email" requesting urgent wire transfer, a "bank call" asking for your OTP — these exploit that conditioning. Establish a verbal code or separate verification channel with colleagues for sensitive requests. A phone call to the real CEO (using a known number, not a provided one) takes 30 seconds.
TRUST

Building False Trust

Using your name, company details, or mutual connections to seem legitimate.

Spear phishers research targets on LinkedIn and social media before attacking. An email that mentions your team's recent project, your manager's name, or your company event feels trustworthy. But that data is public. Always verify requests involving money, credentials, or access — regardless of how personal the email feels.
FEAR

Fear and Threats

"We have your browsing history." Embarrassment drives irrational action.

Sextortion scams claim to have compromising photos or videos and demand cryptocurrency. These are almost always bluffs — the attacker has nothing. They may include one of your old passwords (from public data breaches) to make the threat seem credible. Do not pay. Report to your local cybercrime unit.
REWARD

Too-Good-To-Be-True Offers

"You've won a prize!" Greed is as exploitable as fear.

Lottery wins, gift card rewards, job offers with unusually high salaries — these lures get people to click before thinking. A core rule: if you didn't enter a contest, you didn't win. If a job pays 3× market rate for basic work, it's a scam. Legitimate rewards don't require you to "verify" personal information or pay a "processing fee."
04 — Your Defence

Best Practices Checklist

Check off each practice you're already doing — or commit to starting it. Click each item to mark it complete.

  • Enable MFA on all important accounts
  • Hover over links before clicking to inspect the URL
  • Check sender email domains character by character
  • Never enter credentials on a site you arrived at via email
  • Report suspicious emails to IT / security team
  • Keep browser and OS fully updated
  • Use a password manager — different passwords everywhere
  • Verify wire transfer requests via phone (known number)
  • Don't open attachments from unexpected emails
  • Be sceptical of ANY request for urgency or secrecy
